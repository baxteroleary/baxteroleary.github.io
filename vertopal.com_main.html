Proof by induction: first check if the statement is true for a specific number. This is called the base of induction or the base case. Then, in the proof’s main part, we assume that the statement is true for some number \(n\). This is the inductive hypothesis or inductive assumption. Finally, we use this assumption to show that it is also true for the successor \(n+1\). This is the induction step.

Let \((a_n)\) be a sequence of real numbers. We say \((a_n)\) converges to the limit \(L \in \mathbf{R}\)
as n tends to infitnity, if for all \(\epsilon > 0\) there exists an index \(N = N(\epsilon)\) such that
\(|a_n-L| < \epsilon\) for all \(n > N\).
limit comparison: For two sequences with \(a_n \ge b_n\) then
i) If \(lim_n\to\infty a_n = a\) and \(lim_{n\to\infty} b_n =b\) then \(a\ge b\)
ii) If \(lim_{n\to\infty}b_N = \infty\), then \(lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = \infty\)
Sandwich/Squeeze Rule: Let \((a_n)\) and \((b_n)\) be sequences
with \(lim_{n\to\infty}a_n = \lim_{n\to\infty}c_n = L\). Assume further that for a sequence \((b_n)\)
we have \(a_n \le b_n \le c_n\), Then \((b_n)\) converges and \(lim_{n\to\infty}b_n = L\)

Vamishing Test: Consider \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}a_k\) If the infinity series converges then \(lim_{k\to\infty}a_k = 0\). Can only use this test to prove that a series diverges
An infinite series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) is called absolutely convergent if the series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty |a_k|\) converges.
Comparison Test: Let \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty b_k\) be a series with positive terms that converges. Furthermore let \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) be an infinite series such that \(|a_k| < c\cdot b_k\) for all k with some \(c \in \mathbf{R}\) Then the series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) is absolutely convergent
The infinite series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{k^\alpha}\), with \(\alpha > 0\) converges for \(\alpha > 1\) and diverges for \(\alpha \le 1\)
Asymptotic comparison test: Let \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) and \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty b_k\) be two series with \(a_k,b_k > 0\) and assume that \(\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{a_k}{b_k} = L > 0\) Then the two series either both converge or both diverge to infinity
Ratio Test: Consider \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) and assume that the limit \(L = \lim{k\to\infty}\frac{|a_{k+1}}{|a_k|}\) exists. Then If \(L < 1\), the series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) converges absolutely. If \(L > 1\) the series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k\) diverges.

Planes can be characterised in the following ways:
1. A point P and two vectors \(\vec{v_1}\) and \(\vec{v_2}\) determine a plane, containing P and parallel to both \(\vec{v_1}\) and \(\vec{v_2}\), as long as \(\vec{v_1}\) and \(\vec{v_2}\) point in different directions.
2. Three points \(P_1, P_2, P_3\) determine a plane that contains all three points, as long as the points do not lie in a straight line
3. A point P and vector \(\vec{n}\) determine the plane which goes through P and for which all vectors in the plane are perpendicular to \(\vec{n}\)
Corresponding equations of planes
1. for any point Q in the plane we must have \(\overrightarrow{OQ} = \overrightarrow{OP} + s \vec{v_1} + t \vec{v_2}, \quad s,t \in \mathbb{R}\).
2. When given three points the parametric equation is \(\overrightarrow{OQ} = \overrightarrow{OP_1} + s \cdot \overrightarrow{P_1P_2} + t \cdot \overrightarrow{P_1P_3}, \quad s,t \in \mathbb{R}.\)
3. If the vector \(\vec{n}\) is perpendicular to the plane then this means it is perpendicular to all vectors in the plane. So, let \(Q = (x,y,z)\) be a point on the plane. Then the vector \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\) lies in the plane so we have \(\overrightarrow{PQ} \cdot \vec{n}=0\).
If we know \(\vec{n}\) and P, then the cartesian equation reads \(n_1 x + n_2 y + n_3 z = n_1 p_1 + n_2 p_2 + n_3 p_3.\)
Given any plane S whose equation in Cartesian form is \(a_1 x + a_1y + a_3 z=b\), the vector \((a_1,a_2,a_3)\) is a normal vector for S
